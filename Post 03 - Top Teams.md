# The Team That Wins the City

*One crest per city. Across 237 metros, the team that wins the civic identity contest is rarely the one with the most trophies. It's the one whose disappearance would change what the city is.*

---

A city is a contested noun. It contains commuters who have been there three years, families that have been there four generations, a stock exchange, a language nobody outside it can read, two airports, four newspapers nobody buys, and a team whose colors get pulled out of the closet on Saturdays.

The team is the only one of those that almost everybody can agree on.

This is the second piece in the [Global Metro Power Rankings](https://rankings.citizenofnowhere.org) series. The first piece measured what cities have. This one measures something stranger: which sporting franchise *belongs* to the city in a way that doesn't dilute when you ask the question carefully. The full taxonomy, every row, sits at [rankings.citizenofnowhere.org/top-teams](https://rankings.citizenofnowhere.org/top-teams). Across 237 metros that have a serious case for any answer at all, I walked through the question one row at a time.

The standard for "top team" was deliberately not the obvious one. It is not the team with the most trophies in the metro right now. It is not the team that won most recently. It is the team that, if it disappeared, would change the city's idea of itself. That standard is sharper than it sounds. It rules out the new oligarch project. It rules out the moneyed superclub that arrived after the city already had one. It also, occasionally, rules out the team that has won the most.

Three observations the data forces, before any of the deep dives.

## The Football Default

Out of 237 metros that landed a top-team call, 129 chose a football (soccer) club. Inside the global top 100, football wins fifty-five percent of the time. Outside the top 100, it wins more than three quarters. Football is not the world's most popular sport because of trophies. It is the world's most popular sport because, almost everywhere outside a narrow strip of North America, East Asia, and the cricket-playing Commonwealth, football is the *first* sport a city builds civic life around. The default position is not a tie. It is a settled inheritance.

This is most visible in the places where it isn't football. The carve-outs are tighter than I expected.

In the top fifty metros, twenty-three picked something other than football. Of those twenty-three, twenty-one map cleanly onto a single piece of cultural geography. The American East Coast and Midwest pick American football or baseball. Japan picks baseball. Sydney picks rugby league. Melbourne picks Australian rules. Toronto picks hockey. Mumbai and Delhi pick T20 cricket. Manila picks basketball. Moscow picks basketball. Berlin picks basketball.

The exceptions to the football default trace a more interesting map than the rule. They are an inheritance map. Where the British Empire left cricket and the Royal Navy didn't, you get cricket. Where American expansion routed through baseball clubs and YMCAs in the late nineteenth century, you get baseball. Where Australia's federation turned a Melbourne winter game into the national obsession, you get Aussie rules. The football default is not a verdict. It is the absence of a stronger inheritance.

## Inheritance Versus Insurgency

The hardest calls in the dataset were not the metros where the choice was unclear between sports. They were the metros where two clubs in the same sport had a serious civic claim, and where the deciding question was whether trophies bought yesterday count as much as colors worn since 1880.

London is the canonical case. Arsenal sits on thirteen English league titles, fourteen FA Cups (the most ever), the 2003-04 Invincibles, and the longest unbroken run in the English top flight. Chelsea sits on six English league titles, two Champions League titles, two Europa League titles, and the most European silverware of any London club. Tottenham is third, with two league titles and a last one in 1961. Arsenal is the inheritance pick. Chelsea is the insurgency pick. The piece on the wall in my draft says they are co-equal, and I think they are: Arsenal on continuity and global fanbase, Chelsea on European pedigree and modern trophy density.

Milan is the same problem in cleaner geometry. Internazionale and AC Milan are tied on Serie A titles within a single trophy of each other (twenty against nineteen). Inter has the 2010 Champions League treble. AC Milan has seven European Cups, second only to Real Madrid. They share the San Siro. They share the city. The list does not pick.

Istanbul stands on the Bosphorus and contains two clubs that do not share anything voluntarily. Galatasaray (twenty-five Süper Lig titles, the 2000 UEFA Cup) is Asian-side institutional gravity. Fenerbahçe (nineteen titles, the deepest Turkish supporter base) is European-side civic religion. Beşiktaş trails both with sixteen and a different kind of grief. The list runs Galatasaray and Fenerbahçe co-equal, and that's because in Istanbul, the answer to "whose city is this" depends entirely on which side of the water you ask the question on.

Glasgow is darker. Celtic and Rangers are not co-equal because they have similar trophy counts (they do; fifty-five and fifty-five-ish in the Scottish league depending on how you treat the Rangers reformation). They are co-equal because the question of which one is the "real" Glasgow club is the question Glasgow has spent two centuries refusing to answer. The Old Firm split is sectarian, then political, then class, then geographic, in some order. The right answer is to give them both the slot and to leave a small note in the rationale acknowledging that you cannot read this row without reading the city's twentieth century.

Montevideo's Peñarol against Nacional is the South American twin of the same problem, calmer in tone but identical in shape. Casablanca's Wydad against Raja, Accra's Hearts of Oak against Asante Kotoko, Rosario's Newell's against Central. Where two clubs run side by side in the same metro, the answer is rarely a trophy count. It is whichever one keeps the city's identity legible when the other is gone.

## When the Capital's Flagship Underwhelms

Capital cities tend to produce dominant clubs because political and economic gravity collects there. Real Madrid sits in Madrid for the same reason the Bank of Spain sits in Madrid. PSG dominates Paris because, since 2011, French football has been a Qatari capital project, and Paris was the only metro where that project could land. Beijing Guoan, FC Seoul, Galatasaray, Internazionale, Real Madrid, Real Madrid, Real Madrid: a list of capital flagships traces a list of countries that resolved the question of where political and sporting power consolidate.

The interesting capitals are the ones where the answer is not flat-footed. Berlin's basketball pick is the cleanest example. Berlin is Germany's capital, but it is not Germany's footballing capital, which is somewhere between Munich and the Ruhr. Hertha BSC is a relegated, debt-laden mess. Union Berlin is a rising story but not yet a civic monument. Alba Berlin, on the other hand, is a German basketball dynasty with eleven Bundesliga titles. Berlin chose basketball because the football inheritance never settled into a single club, and the next sport down had a better claim.

Moscow's CSKA pick is the same logic with a longer Russian history behind it. Spartak Moscow has ten Soviet and ten Russian league titles in football. PBC CSKA has eight Euroleague titles, tied with Real Madrid for the most in European basketball. Either is defensible. I went CSKA because Russian sport has spent the better part of a century operating through state-owned multi-sport clubs (CSKA, Dynamo, Lokomotiv), and CSKA Moscow is the cleanest export the model has produced. Spartak fans will object. They are entitled to.

Hong Kong is the failure mode of the same logic. Kitchee SC is the best Hong Kong has, with nine Hong Kong Premier League titles and the only HK club to reach the AFC Champions League group stage. Hong Kong is also a metro that ranks eighteenth globally on the composite, ahead of Milan and Madrid. The gap between Hong Kong's civic stature and its top sporting franchise's continental footprint is one of the largest in the dataset. Some cities are great cities that don't yet have a great team. Hong Kong is one of them. So is Singapore, where Albirex Niigata Singapore (a Japanese B-team running the S-League) sits in the slot for entirely structural reasons.

## The Small Market Holds the Soul

The Milwaukee row reads "Green Bay Packers." This is not an error. Milwaukee is a Wisconsin metro with two top-tier professional franchises, the Brewers and Bucks. Green Bay sits two hours north and is, at 350,000 people, the smallest market in any major North American sports league. The Packers have thirteen NFL championships (the most), four Super Bowl wins, are publicly owned by their fans, play at Lambeau Field in front of 80,000 people in a city of 100,000, and command the cultural emotional weight of all of Wisconsin.

The list slots them under Milwaukee because Milwaukee is the metro and the metro picks the team that anchors it. Green Bay anchors Wisconsin. Milwaukee, by extension, gets the Packers as its top team and the Brewers and Bucks as runners-up. That is the right answer. It is also a useful reminder that "top team" is a function of cultural footprint, not metro residence. You don't have to live in the city to belong to it.

The Japanese baseball belt repeats the same pattern at smaller scales. The Hiroshima Toyo Carp are not the most decorated team in Nippon Professional Baseball: the Yomiuri Giants and the Yakult Swallows have more rings. The Carp are nonetheless Hiroshima's team in a way that the Giants are not Tokyo's, because Hiroshima is small enough that the Carp matter to it disproportionately. Sapporo's Hokkaido Nippon-Ham Fighters perform the same function on Hokkaido. Fukuoka's SoftBank Hawks. The pattern holds: where the metro is small relative to the league it competes in, the team's civic weight is heavier.

## When the City Is the College

Sixteen rows in the dataset list a college program as the top team. They cluster in two ranges: the deep American South-and-Plains football cradle (Tuscaloosa, Tallahassee, Knoxville, Baton Rouge, College Station, Gainesville, Norman if I had ranked it), and the Carolina basketball axis (Lexington, Louisville, Raleigh-Durham). Every one of these sixteen metros shares the same feature: the city is too small to support a major professional franchise, and the relevant Big-Time-College program operates at a scale that no professional franchise in a city of comparable size could match.

The Tuscaloosa row says Alabama Crimson Tide football. The metro has 250,000 people. Alabama football's home games draw 100,000. The economic shadow of a single Saturday in October dwarfs the city's regular commercial activity. The same is true in Columbus (Ohio State, although Columbus is large enough to have a real metro economy outside football), in Eugene (Oregon), in Lincoln (Nebraska), in State College (Penn State). The Florida State row says Tallahassee. The Notre Dame row says South Bend, a metro of 320,000 whose national-cathedral football program drives every fall conversation about the city.

The Raleigh-Durham row is the only one in the top 100 that runs a college program as a top-team call, and it lists North Carolina and Duke as co-equal. Tobacco Road basketball is the rare college slot where two programs in the same metro are individually national institutions and together form a single civic identity. The rationale ran 675 characters. It is the longest in the dataset. The reason it is the longest is that, unlike the Old Firm or the Milan duopoly, this contest is not bitter. The two schools play each other twice a year and the city stops working both nights. That is what civic religion looks like when it is friendly.

## The 2024-2025 Calibration

A handful of rows almost flipped between drafts because of recent results.

Los Angeles is a Lakers row. The Dodgers won the 2024 World Series and the 2025 World Series, the first repeat champion in MLB in twenty-five years, behind a hundred-million-dollar Ohtani contract that is itself a Los Angeles cultural artifact. The Lakers still hold the slot because seventeen NBA titles, three multi-decade dynasties, and a global brand that intersects Hollywood do not get unseated by two seasons, however dominant. The Dodgers are listed as a co-equal in the rationale. If a third title comes in 2026, the call should flip. Probably the right move now is to make it a Lakers / Dodgers shared row, in the London model.

Sydney is a Penrith Panthers row, and four years ago it would have been a Roosters row. The Panthers won four consecutive NRL premierships in 2021, 2022, 2023, and 2024, the first four-peat in NRL history, behind Nathan Cleary's generational halfback play. The Roosters are still the historic answer with fifteen premierships tied for the most in rugby league. Sydney's call is genuinely a snapshot of the present. It will read differently in 2030.

Tampa is a Tampa Bay Buccaneers row, but Tampa's last fifteen years are, more accurately, a Lightning row. The Lightning won back-to-back Stanley Cups in 2020 and 2021 and went to a third consecutive final in 2022. Hockey in Florida is one of the more improbable cultural shifts of the modern North American sports landscape, and it deserves an honorable mention any time the city is invoked.

The PSG row would have read differently any time before 2025. Paris won its first Champions League title in 2025, after fourteen years of Qatari investment that a lot of European football considered a vanity project. The trophy, finally, makes the row indisputable. Without it the rationale would have been wobblier than its current confidence.

These calibrations matter because the question of which team belongs to a city is not static. It moves under the city's feet at the speed of championships. The dataset captures a moment, not a verdict.

## What the Exercise Actually Reveals

A list like this is interesting in the same way a coat of arms is interesting. It tells you what the city decided to claim publicly when it had to pick one symbol.

Five things become visible when you read the 237-row sheet end to end.

First, the football default is so strong that the carve-outs *are* the cultural map. If you want to know where the British Empire's cricketing afterglow lives, the cricket rows tell you. If you want to know where America's late-nineteenth-century ballpark expansion still echoes, the baseball rows tell you. The exceptions to football are the most informative single layer in the dataset.

Second, the metros where the call is contested are the metros where the city is contested. London, Milan, Istanbul, Glasgow. Two halves of one identity, refusing to consolidate, producing twin franchises that exist in part to keep that refusal visible. The contested rows are the most honest rows in the dataset.

Third, capital cities are not always sporting capitals. Berlin chose basketball. Hong Kong's case is a polite shrug. The political center of a country is not automatically its sporting center, and where it isn't, the city's relationship to its top team gets more interesting, not less. The flagship-club model is a Madrid-and-Paris pattern, not a universal one.

Fourth, civic gravity does not require metro residence. Wisconsin's top team is in a town smaller than a New York neighborhood. Kentucky's top team plays in a city of 320,000. The team's reach is what matters, and reach is decided by the franchise's relationship with the surrounding region, not by which incorporated boundary the stadium sits inside.

Fifth, the project of picking one team per city forces you to admit that "the team" is a fiction, and that the fiction is necessary. New York has the Knicks. The Giants. The Rangers. The Mets. The Yankees rationale runs four lines. Every other team in the metro gets a sentence, sometimes a phrase. The Yankees are the team the city would claim if it had to pick one before walking into a foreign country and explaining where it was from. They are the answer to a question the city asks itself every fifty years and answers, every time, the same way.
