**The Global Metro Power Rankings**


Measuring What Makes a City Matter


*A 16-dimension composite score across 4,285 metropolitan areas, hand-curated over two years, attempts to quantify the completeness of a city’s global infrastructure.*


---

## The Question


Population alone does not make a great city. Plenty of metros have tens of millions of people and little else. GDP alone doesn’t explain it either, because GDP doesn’t capture why people move to Berlin instead of Doha. The more interesting question is which cities have built the most complete ecosystem of infrastructure, culture, sport, finance, education, and connectivity.


I spent two years trying to answer that. I hand-curated data across 16 dimensions for every metropolitan area on Earth: 4,285 of them, spanning 237 countries. No scraping, no AI-generated data dumps. Every museum, every transit system, every stock exchange was individually verified and mapped to a specific metro through a municipality-level geographic hierarchy covering 182,000+ administrative units.


The result is a composite score that I call “metro completeness”: the breadth and depth of globally-recognized infrastructure and activity concentrated in one place.

## What the Score Measures


The composite score sums 16 weighted components:

- **Population** – linear, divided by 3 million
- **Market capitalization** – total corporate HQ value, linear, divided by $700 billion
- **Major league teams** – top-tier professional sports presence, capped at 10
- **Minor/college teams** – 0.25 points each, capped at 40 teams
- **Cultural assets** – museums, landmarks, ports, stock exchanges, IXPs, military bases, central banks, data centers, and agricultural heritage sites (0.65 points each)
- **Airport score** – weighted by global tier (Mega Hub = 5, Major International = 3, International = 2, Regional = 1, Small = 0.5), multiplied by 0.25
- **Top-50 universities** – 3.5 points each (CWUR ranking)
- **Other universities, hospitals, and research institutions** – 2.2 points each
- **Metro transit** – subway and light rail station count, LOG-scaled
- **GaWC**** global connectivity** – reciprocal of world city network rank (12 ÷ rank)
- **Suburban rail** – commuter rail stations, LOG × 0.5
- **Intercity train hubs** – stations with 30M+ annual passengers, LOG × 2.0
- **Skyscrapers** – 150m+ buildings, LOG × 5.7
- **Sporting events** – Olympics, World Cups, Grand Slams hosted (0.2 each, capped at 20)
- **Annual events** – recurring festivals, parades, and fairs (1.0 point each)
- **Luxury hospitality** – Michelin stars and Forbes Travel Guide hotel stars, LOG × 3.0
- **GDP tier** – tiered from 0 to 3 points based on metro economic output

The philosophy behind the weighting is deliberate: **curated categorical data, not raw volume metrics.** An airport’s tier matters more than its passenger count. A city’s number of stock exchanges matters more than its trading volume. The scoring rewards *breadth* (having something across many dimensions) over extreme depth in one.

## Nine Cities Score Above 100


To clear 100, a metro needs strength across nearly every dimension: major sports leagues, world-class universities, a skyline, a transit system, global corporate presence, cultural landmarks, and international events. Only nine metros get there:


New York (175.7) · London (174.5) · Paris (137.8) · Tokyo (128.8) · San Francisco–San Jose (117.6) · Los Angeles (109.0) · Beijing (105.5) · Seoul (104.0) · Shanghai (101.8)

## The San Francisco Anomaly


San Francisco–San Jose at #5 (117.6) is the dataset’s most notable outlier. At 8 million people, it is smaller than Bangkok, Jakarta, or Manila, yet it scores higher than all of them combined.


The explanation is concentrated in a single dimension: **$25.7 trillion in market capitalization**, driven by Apple, Alphabet, Meta, NVIDIA, and the broader Silicon Valley ecosystem. Market cap alone contributes roughly 35 points to SF’s score, which is more than the entire composite score of most metros on Earth.


I consider this a legitimate feature of the model, not a distortion. The scoring treats market cap as a signal of where the world’s most consequential economic activity concentrates. By that measure, the corridor between San Jose and San Francisco represents the densest concentration of corporate value in human history.

## A More Granular View: 11 Regions


The six-continent framework is useful but blunt. It groups Turkey with Indonesia, lumps Russia with France, and files Australia under Asia. The dataset maintains an alternative regional classification that splits the world into 11 blocs, each representing a more culturally and economically coherent grouping: North America, Europe, China, East Asia (Japan, South Korea, Taiwan), MENA, Eurasia (Russia, Central Asia, the Caucasus), Latin America, ASEAN, Oceania, South Asia, and Africa.

A few things become visible here that the traditional breakdown obscures. First, China as its own region is remarkably deep: 175 metros, 4 scoring above 50, and a median score of 2.52, which is the highest of any region. Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou all clear 95. No other non-Western region has three metros at that level.


East Asia (Japan, South Korea, Taiwan), separated from China, reveals a different pattern. It has only 89 metros but its top two, Tokyo (128.8) and Seoul (104.0), are both in the global top 10. The region has extreme concentration at the top and relatively little depth below it.


The Oceania reclassification is notable. Under the standard six-continent model, Australia falls under Asia (the UN geoscheme groups it with Asia-Pacific), which buries Sydney and Melbourne behind Tokyo, Seoul, Beijing, and Shanghai. Reassigned to Oceania, Sydney (71.6) and Melbourne (60.9) emerge as clear regional leaders. Auckland (25.6), which led Oceania in the traditional view, drops to a distant fourth behind Brisbane (30.2).


Eurasia is the most structurally top-heavy region in the dataset: 442 metros, but only one (Moscow) scores above 50, and only two score above 20. The median score is 0.09, the lowest of any region. This is largely an artifact of the number of small Central Asian and Caucasus metros with minimal global infrastructure.


Africa remains the only region with zero metros scoring above 50. Johannesburg leads at 44.3, followed by Cape Town (26.8) and Nairobi (20.9). Of 282 metros, just 3 score above 20.


ASEAN, despite being the smallest region by metro count after Oceania, has a notably flat top: Singapore (62.0), Bangkok (52.2), and Kuala Lumpur (50.6) are all within 12 points of each other. That kind of competitive parity at the top is unusual in the dataset. Most regions are defined by a dominant capital or two, with a steep dropoff. ASEAN has three cities operating at roughly the same tier.

## New York vs. London: 1.2 Points, Two Different Models of Dominance


I should disclose a bias. I have lived in both cities, moving between them four times since 2011. London was my foundation: the better part of a decade in Clerkenwell, building a career in the UK tech scene through roles at companies like Adform and VisualDNA. New York was the acceleration: two stints, the first from late 2017 through 2020 focused on data strategy (with OnePublicisTeamSamsung and Spotify) the second from 2022 to early 2025 on the Upper West Side, working at Amazon Advertising and sports (with time at the NFL and MLB in New York). I am currently back in London, in Marylebone, which gives me a different vantage point on the city than my years in EC1 did.


When I tell people the dataset has New York at 175.7 and London at 174.5, the first question is always the same: how are they that close? The answer, once you decompose the scores, is that they are close *despite* being radically different cities. They arrive at nearly the same number through almost completely non-overlapping strengths.

New York’s scoring profile is defined by vertical scale. It has 347 skyscrapers to London’s 44, which translates to an 11.7-point advantage in that dimension alone. Its market capitalization advantage is even larger: $8.7 trillion versus $4.2 trillion, contributing 6.3 more points. The New York subway has 499 stations, the largest rapid transit system in the Western Hemisphere. Its GDP of $2.5 trillion would make it the eighth-largest national economy on Earth if it were a country. New York is a city that builds upward and accumulates corporate mass.


London’s profile is defined by institutional breadth. It has 53 museums, landmarks, bridges, and canals to New York’s 35, producing a 9.1-point cultural advantage. Its suburban rail network of 1,023 stations (the Overground, Thameslink, Southeastern, and the National Rail commuter services) is 2.5 times larger than New York’s. It has 15 intercity train hubs with 30M+ annual passengers to New York’s 3. Its luxury hospitality footprint (265 Michelin and Forbes stars) exceeds New York’s by 60%. And London has hosted 30 major international sporting events, double New York’s 15, a reflection of the Olympics, multiple Rugby and Cricket World Cups, Wimbledon’s annual cadence, and the sheer density of Premier League football. Seven clubs play in the top flight. New York has two MLS teams.


The structural difference maps neatly onto the broader North America vs. Europe dynamic in the dataset. North America has 686 metros to Europe’s 1,385, but its aggregate market capitalization ($80.4 trillion) is triple Europe’s ($26.5 trillion). North America has 1,133 skyscrapers to Europe’s 224. It has 13 metros scoring above 50, compared to Europe’s 7. Yet Europe has 118 intercity train hubs to North America’s 15, 9,260 suburban rail stations to North America’s 1,461, and 431 museums and landmarks to North America’s 330. Europe’s infrastructure is older, more distributed, and more layered into the urban fabric. North America’s is newer, more concentrated, and more vertically oriented.


One number crystallizes the difference in how these two regions concentrate their power. London accounts for 40.9% of the United Kingdom’s total metro score. New York accounts for 7.3% of the United States’. London *is* the UK, in a way that no single American city is America. The US distributes its infrastructure across a dozen major metros; the UK funnels an extraordinary proportion of its national capacity through a single node. This is a pattern visible across most of Europe: Paris dominates France, Milan and Madrid anchor Italy and Spain, and there are steep dropoffs after the capital in nearly every European country. The American model, by contrast, produces depth: six US metros score above 80, and thirteen score above 50.


Having lived this duality for fifteen years, I can say the data reflects something that is genuinely felt on the ground. New York’s intensity is economic and vertical. You feel the corporate density, the sheer volume of transactions and people, the skyline asserting itself. London’s intensity is institutional and layered. You feel the depth of history, the transport network threading through neighborhoods that have been continuously inhabited for centuries, the cultural infrastructure embedded into the streetscape in a way that resists quantification but that the scoring does, I think, capture reasonably well. The 1.2-point gap between them is not convergence. It is two fundamentally different strategies for building a world city, arriving at the same destination by entirely different routes.

## Methodology Notes


**Data sources:** All data is curated from official, first-party sources: IATA, ACI, CTBUH, Michelin Guide, Forbes Travel Guide, CWUR university rankings, GaWC world city network, companiesmarketcap.com, Wikipedia transit data, and extensive manual research for cultural assets, sporting events, and infrastructure. No third-party aggregators were used for financial data.


**Geographic precision:** Every data point is mapped to a specific municipality and aggregated upward through a hand-built hierarchy of 133,000+ municipalities (33 countries) and 49,000+ counties (237 countries). Metro boundaries are defined bottom-up from core urban clusters, not from external metro definitions (OECD, Brookings, etc.) that use incompatible geographic boundaries.


**What’s deliberately excluded:** Raw volume metrics (passenger counts, container TEU, metro-level GDP) are not used. Metro-level GDP from external sources uses different geographic boundaries than this dataset. Market capitalization serves as the economic weight proxy. Football (soccer) league data is maintained separately and feeds into the scoring via local data tables.


**Known limitations:** English-language source bias means Africa and South Asia are structurally undercounted in cultural categories. Michelin Guide coverage only extends to select countries. The scoring captures a current snapshot with no temporal dimension.
